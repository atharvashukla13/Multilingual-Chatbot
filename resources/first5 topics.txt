Project Title and Domain
Proposed Title: Bilingual Ayurvedic Health Advisor Chatbot (English-Hindi)
Domain: Conversational AI for healthcare, specifically Ayurvedic wellness and lifestyle advice. Ayurveda is a specialized traditional medical system with rich, culturally grounded knowledge; mainstream models often underperform without domain adaptation[1]. A chatbot in this domain would leverage multilingual NLP to explain Ayurvedic concepts (doshas, herbs, treatments) in user-friendly English and Hindi.
Problem Statement / Research Motivation
Despite India’s linguistic diversity, many conversational AI systems remain monolingual or narrowly specialized[2]. This creates a barrier when providing health information, especially in traditional domains like Ayurveda. Language barriers can worsen health inequities, as seen in clinical studies where limited-English-proficiency patients had poorer outcomes without multilingual support[3]. Meanwhile, rural and non-English-speaking communities often lack easy access to reliable Ayurvedic guidance. Building a bilingual Ayurvedic chatbot addresses these gaps: it breaks the one-language-at-a-time limitation[2], offers equitable health communication[3], and brings ancient medical wisdom to modern users. Moreover, because Ayurveda’s knowledge is nuanced and culturally embedded, a dedicated system must capture domain-specific terms and reasoning[1]. In summary, the motivation is to enable accessible, culturally congruent health advice by combining multilingual NLP with Ayurveda’s rich textual heritage.
Objectives of the Project
•	Multilingual Dialogue Capability: Develop a conversational agent that understands and responds in English and Hindi, handling queries and code-mixed (Hinglish) input.
•	Ayurvedic Knowledge Integration: Curate or construct a question-answer and knowledge base from Ayurvedic texts and trusted health sources to feed the chatbot, ensuring factual Ayurvedic content.
•	Transformer-based NLP Models: Fine-tune or train multilingual transformer models (e.g. MuRIL or domain-adapted LLMs) on the Ayurvedic dataset for intent recognition and answer generation[4][1].
•	Contextual Understanding: Implement intent classification and dialogue management that capture Ayurvedic context (e.g. dosha types, symptoms) and use cross-lingual or translation techniques only if necessary, to avoid large translation overhead[5].
•	User Interface and Accessibility: Provide a user-friendly chat interface (text and optional voice) that can handle speech-to-text in both languages, catering to users with varying literacy.
•	Evaluation and Feedback: Measure system performance with metrics like intent classification F1, answer relevance (BLEU/METEOR for translated responses), and conduct user studies for satisfaction. Ensure iterative improvement based on user feedback and healthcare experts’ review[6][1].
Literature Survey / Related Work
Multilingual Chatbots and NLP Techniques: Recent studies emphasize the need for multilingual bot frameworks. Kasinathan et al. propose a customizable system to deploy chatbots in multiple languages, noting that many existing services support only one language[2]. Similarly, Singh et al. build an Indian-language chatbot by fine-tuning transformer models (e.g. MuRIL BERT) for question-answering, avoiding heavy machine translation pipelines[4][5]. These works show that directly training on multilingual corpora (rather than translating on-the-fly) yields better performance and lower latency for Indian languages. Anastasiou et al. also highlight this for Luxembourgish, creating a manual dataset of dialog questions and using a BERT-based QA model to enable under-resourced language support[7]. Overall, the literature suggests that multilingual bots can leverage pre-trained cross-lingual embeddings and fine-tuning to understand intent across languages without full translation cycles.
Healthcare and Under-Resourced Language Chatbots: Healthcare is a prominent use-case for multilingual bots. Badlani et al. demonstrate a healthcare chatbot supporting English, Hindi, and Gujarati for rural India. Their system uses TF-IDF and similarity matching to diagnose based on symptoms, reporting high accuracy (∼98%) and including speech-to-text to aid low-literacy users[8][9]. This shows that adding local language support drastically increases utility in underserved areas. Rainey et al. similarly find that a multilingual SMS chatbot significantly improved engagement and reduced readmissions for patients with limited English proficiency[3]. These studies motivate an Ayurvedic bot: like disease diagnosis bots, an Ayurvedic advisor should handle symptom queries and FAQs in the patient’s preferred language.
Cross-Cultural and Code-Switching Considerations: Orosoo et al. explore enhancing NLP in multilingual bots to handle idioms and cultural context, using techniques like phonetic analysis and transfer learning (e.g. MUSE embeddings) for cross-cultural communication[6]. This is relevant because Ayurvedic terminology often carries cultural nuance (Sanskrit terms, local health beliefs). Handling code-switching (mixing Hindi and English) is also crucial. Prior work indicates that simple translation pipelines may fail for code-switched queries, and that intent models must be robust to mixed-language input[5]. Therefore, the chatbot should include a language identification or unified model layer to interpret Hinglish inputs natively.
Translation vs. Transformer Models: Classical methods rely on machine translation (MT) to map all languages into one. However, many have moved to multilingual transformers to reduce MT overhead. Singh et al. note that MT is “expensive” at run time and instead fine-tune a transformer to directly match queries in any language to English knowledge-base answers[5]. This approach is promising for Ayurvedic advice: using a model like MuRIL or a domain-tuned LLM can capture semantic intent in Hindi or English directly, mapping to the Ayurvedic corpus.
Overall, related work shows that building a bilingual health chatbot involves combining multilingual NLP (fine-tuned transformers, cross-lingual embeddings) with domain-specific corpora[4][1]. Prior successes in healthcare and low-resource languages underscore the feasibility and guide the system design for an Ayurvedic chatbot.
Scope and Limitations
•	Scope: The chatbot targets general users in India seeking Ayurvedic health advice. It will support English and Hindi (Devanagari script) for queries and responses. The domain includes common wellness topics (diet, lifestyle, symptom inquiry, herbal remedies) within Ayurvedic knowledge. The system will operate as a question-answer and conversational agent (likely text-based, with optional voice I/O) that explains concepts in simple language. It will not attempt formal medical diagnosis or treatment planning beyond general advice, nor will it replace professional consultation.
•	Limitations: Accurate Ayurvedic advice requires high-quality domain data, which is currently scarce; building and verifying a robust Ayurvedic corpus is challenging. The language model may struggle with code-mixed Hinglish queries or rare dialectal usage. Pre-trained LMs also risk factual errors or “hallucinations,” especially in a specialized medical context[1], so the bot must signal uncertainty and encourage professional help. Evaluation of health chatbots is difficult, often relying on subjective user satisfaction or qualitative analysis. Finally, ethical considerations (privacy, disclaimers, biases) impose constraints: the chatbot must explicitly avoid harmful recommendations and clarify that it is an informational tool, not a licensed practitioner.
Sources: Key insights were drawn from recent multilingual chatbot research focusing on healthcare and low-resource languages[2][4][8][3][1], which informed the problem definition, objectives, and scope above.
